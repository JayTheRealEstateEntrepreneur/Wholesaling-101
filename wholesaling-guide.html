Introduction

Introduction to Real Estate Wholesaling

Everything you need to know about what wholesaling is, how it actually works, and whether it's the right path for you.

What Is Real Estate Wholesaling?

Real estate wholesaling is a short-term investment strategy where you find a property being sold below market value, get it under contract with the seller, and then sell your interest in that contract — not the property itself — to a cash buyer for a profit. You act as the middleman connecting motivated sellers with active investors.

The key distinction: you are not buying the property. You are buying the right to purchase it (your equitable interest in the contract), then selling that right to someone else before the closing date. The profit you earn — called an assignment fee or wholesale fee — is the difference between the price you locked in with the seller and the price your buyer agrees to pay.

💡
Simple Example

You find a distressed home worth $200,000 (ARV). You negotiate a purchase price of $120,000 with the seller. You market the deal to your buyers list and find an investor who agrees to purchase the contract for $135,000. At closing, the investor pays $135,000 — the seller gets $120,000 — and you receive $15,000 as your assignment fee.

How Does Wholesaling Actually Work? (Step by Step)

  1. Find a motivated seller — someone who needs to sell quickly due to foreclosure, divorce, job loss, inherited property, or property distress.
  2. Analyze the property — research the ARV (After Repair Value), estimate repair costs, and determine your MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer).
  3. Negotiate and make an offer — present your cash offer to the seller. If they accept, you proceed to contract.
  4. Get the property under contract — sign a purchase agreement that includes an assignment clause, giving you the right to assign your interest to a third party.
  5. Market the deal — send the deal details to your buyers list, investor groups, and social media.
  6. Find a cash buyer — a real estate investor agrees to purchase the contract for a higher price than you locked in.
  7. Sign an assignment agreement — you formally assign your contract rights to the buyer for an agreed assignment fee.
  8. Close — the title company or closing attorney handles the transaction. You collect your fee.

Why Do People Wholesale?

✅ Pros of Wholesaling

  • Low startup capital required — you don't need to buy the property, so you don't need a mortgage or hundreds of thousands of dollars.
  • No credit needed — since you're not financing a purchase, your credit score doesn't affect your ability to do deals.
  • Fast cash — wholesale deals can close in as little as 2–4 weeks, making it one of the fastest ways to generate income in real estate.
  • Learn the market — wholesaling forces you to deeply understand ARV, rehab costs, and investor psychology — priceless education for any future investing.
  • Scalable — with systems, VAs, and a strong buyers list, you can do multiple deals per month without significant overhead.
  • No landlord headaches — you're in and out of deals quickly with no tenants or property management to deal with.

❌ Cons of Wholesaling

  • Inconsistent income — deals don't close every week. Income can be feast-or-famine, especially as a beginner.
  • Rejection-heavy — you'll hear "no" far more than "yes." Cold calling and prospecting require thick skin.
  • Competitive market — many markets are saturated with wholesalers chasing the same leads.
  • Legal complexity — wholesaling laws vary by state. Some states have enacted laws requiring real estate licenses to wholesale.
  • No passive income — unlike rental properties, wholesaling requires active, ongoing work.
  • Deal fall-through risk — if your buyer backs out or financing falls through, your deal could collapse.
⚠️
Know Your State Laws
Some states — including Illinois, Oklahoma, and others — have passed laws requiring a real estate license to wholesale or restricting how properties can be marketed before closing. Always consult a real estate attorney in your state before getting started.

Wholesaling is a volume game that requires consistent, daily action in the face of rejection, silence, and setbacks. The people who win are not the most talented. They are the most persistent.

The 5 Biggest Mindset Mistakes Beginners Make

1. Paralysis by Analysis

Many beginners spend months studying wholesaling without ever contacting a single seller. Knowledge without action is worthless. You learn wholesaling by doing it, not by studying it endlessly. Commit to making your first 100 contacts before you decide if it's working.

2. Quitting After the First "No"

You will get rejected. A lot. Sellers will hang up on you, ignore your letters, and tell you that your offer is insulting. This is normal. The wholesalers who succeed treat rejection as data, not as a personal attack. Keep score of your rejections — they are proof you are doing the work.

3. Chasing Shiny Objects

There are a thousand "gurus" selling a thousand "systems." Most beginners jump from strategy to strategy instead of mastering one approach. Pick a primary lead source — cold calling, direct mail, or driving for dollars — and work it consistently for 90 days before adding another method.

4. Underestimating Marketing Costs

Wholesaling is not free. Direct mail, skip tracing, software, and marketing materials cost real money. Budget at least $500–$1,000/month for marketing in the early stages. If your budget is limited, focus on free methods: cold calling with free county records, Facebook outreach, and networking.

5. Not Building a Buyers List First

Many beginners focus entirely on finding deals, then scramble to find buyers. Build your buyers list simultaneously. Start attending local REIA meetups and adding investor contacts before you ever find your first deal.

⭐
Jerrold's Daily Mindset Ritual
Every morning before prospecting, remind yourself: "My job today is to make contacts. Not to close deals." This removes the pressure and keeps you focused on what you can control — your activity. Deals follow consistent activity. Trust the process.

The Non-Negotiable Habits of Successful Wholesalers

  • 📞 Daily prospecting — Contact new leads every single business day without exception
  • 📋 Follow-up system — 80% of deals come from follow-up, not first contact
  • 📊 Track your numbers — Know your contact-to-offer and offer-to-deal ratios at all times
  • 🤝 Network constantly — Attend REIAs, connect on Facebook groups, build relationships with investors
  • 📚 Learn daily — Read, listen to podcasts, study your market's comps and sold data
  • 💬 Talk to other wholesalers — Find a mentor or accountability partner in your market

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Step 1: Find a Motivated Seller

This is where wholesaling begins and ends. Everything else is secondary to your ability to find sellers who NEED to sell — not just want to sell. Motivated sellers include people facing foreclosure, divorce, probate, tax delinquency, vacancy, code violations, or extreme property distress. We cover all 25+ lead generation methods in Chapter 5.

Step 2: Analyze the Property

Once you have a lead, do your homework. Pull comparable sales (comps) within a half-mile radius, check the property's condition, and research any liens or title issues. You're building the foundation for your offer. Chapter 8 teaches the full deal analysis process.

Step 3: Estimate Repair Costs

Your buyer is an investor who will renovate the property. You need to estimate what those renovations will cost so you can price the deal appropriately. You don't need exact contractor bids — you need a solid ballpark using standard cost-per-square-foot guides. Chapter 8 includes repair cost ranges.

Step 4: Calculate ARV (After Repair Value)

ARV is what the property will be worth after it's fully renovated. This is derived from comparable sales of similar, updated homes in the same area. Your ARV drives every other number in the deal. A wrong ARV = a wrong offer.

Step 5: Determine Your MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer)

MAO = ARV × 70% − Estimated Repairs. This formula ensures your buyer has enough margin to renovate, hold, and profit from the deal — while leaving room for your assignment fee. Chapter 8 has full worked examples.

Step 6: Negotiate with the Seller

Present your offer professionally and empathetically. Your goal is a win-win: the seller gets the fast, hassle-free sale they need, and you get a deal with enough margin for your fee and your buyer's profit. Chapter 9 covers negotiation deeply.

Step 7: Get the Property Under Contract

If the seller accepts, you sign a purchase agreement that includes an assignment clause. This contract gives you equitable interest in the property and the right to assign that interest to another buyer. You also provide a small earnest money deposit to show good faith.

Step 8: Market the Deal

Email your buyers list, post in investor Facebook groups, and call your top buyers. Include the property address, asking price, estimated ARV, estimated repairs, and the deposit amount. Give your buyers 24–72 hours to respond before going wider.

Step 9: Find and Qualify Your Cash Buyer

Review your offers, verify proof of funds, and select the strongest buyer. Make sure they can close within your contract's closing window. Chapter 11 covers how to build a buyers list and qualify buyers.

Step 10: Sign the Assignment Contract

You and the buyer sign an assignment agreement that formally transfers your contract rights to them in exchange for your assignment fee. The title company receives both the original purchase agreement and the assignment agreement.

Step 11: Close at the Title Company

The title company or closing attorney handles the closing. The buyer pays the full purchase price. The seller receives their net proceeds. You receive your assignment fee — often disbursed directly from the closing.

Step 12: Get Paid!

Your fee arrives via check or wire transfer at or shortly after closing. Average fees range from $5,000 to $20,000+ depending on your market and deal size.

Real Estate Basics

Real Estate Basics Every Wholesaler Must Know

You don't need a real estate degree — but you do need to understand these core concepts before you ever make an offer.

After Repair Value (ARV)

ARV is the estimated market value of a property after it has been fully renovated to current market standards. It is the single most important number in any wholesale deal. Get it right, and your deal works. Get it wrong, and your deal falls apart — or your buyer loses money and never works with you again.

How to calculate ARV: Pull 3–5 comparable sales (comps) of similar homes within a half-mile radius that sold within the last 90 days. Adjust for square footage, bedroom/bathroom count, lot size, and condition. Your ARV is the average — or conservative estimate — of those comps.

📊
Best Free ARV Tools
Zillow (use Zestimates as a starting point only), Redfin (better comp data), county assessor websites, and the MLS through a licensed agent contact. For paid tools, PropStream and BatchLeads provide comp data.

Comparable Sales (Comps)

Comps are recently sold properties similar to the subject property in location, size, style, and condition. The key word is sold — not listed. Active listings tell you what people want to get. Sold comps tell you what buyers actually paid.

The perfect comp: Within 0.5 miles · Sold within 90 days · Within 20% of square footage · Same number of beds/baths · Similar style (ranch to ranch, colonial to colonial) · Fully updated condition

Equity

Equity is the difference between what a property is worth and what is owed on it. A property worth $200,000 with a $50,000 mortgage has $150,000 in equity. As a wholesaler, you're looking for distressed equity — situations where the owner has equity but can't or won't access it through traditional channels.

Liens

A lien is a legal claim against a property. Liens must typically be paid off at or before closing. Common types:

Lien TypeDescriptionImpact on Deal
Mortgage LienOwed to the lender from purchase financingPaid off at closing from sale proceeds
Tax LienOwed to IRS or state for unpaid taxesMust be paid to clear title
Property Tax LienUnpaid property taxes to countyOften must be paid at closing
Mechanic's LienFiled by contractors for unpaid workMust negotiate or pay to close
HOA LienUnpaid homeowners association feesMust be resolved at closing
Judgment LienCourt-ordered debt from a lawsuitAttaches to property; must be cleared
⚠️
Always Check for Liens
Run a preliminary title search on every property before getting under contract. Your title company can do this. Undisclosed liens can kill a deal at closing — or wipe out your fee entirely. Never skip this step.

Title

Title is the legal right to own and use a property. "Clear title" means there are no unresolved ownership disputes, liens, or encumbrances. Your buyer's title company or closing attorney will run a title search to confirm this. As a wholesaler, you want to ensure the properties you put under contract will be able to close with clear title.

Escrow

Escrow is a neutral third-party arrangement where money or documents are held until the conditions of a transaction are met. At closing, the escrow or title company holds all funds and releases them to the appropriate parties once all paperwork is signed and conditions are satisfied.

Earnest Money

Earnest money (also called a good-faith deposit) is a small deposit you provide to the seller when signing a purchase agreement. It shows the seller you are serious about buying. If you back out without a valid contingency, you typically forfeit this deposit.

Wholesaling earnest money strategy: Keep it as low as possible ($100–$1,000) while still making the deal credible. Many sellers accept low earnest money, especially if they're motivated. Your offer price and timeline matter more to them than the deposit amount.

Repair Cost Estimates

As a wholesaler, you need to estimate rehab costs accurately enough to price the deal correctly for your buyer. You don't need exact bids. Use these general ranges:

Rehab LevelConditionCost per Sq FtExample (1,200 sq ft)
LightPaint, flooring, fixtures, landscaping$15–$30$18K–$36K
MediumKitchen, bath updates, HVAC tune-up$30–$60$36K–$72K
HeavyFull gut rehab, roof, foundation, systems$60–$100+$72K–$120K+

Closing Costs

Closing costs are fees paid at the closing of a real estate transaction. In a wholesale deal, your buyer (not you) typically pays the majority of closing costs. Common items include:

  • Title search and title insurance: 0.5%–1% of purchase price
  • Attorney or escrow fees: $500–$2,000
  • Recording fees: $50–$500
  • Transfer taxes: varies by state/county
  • Prorated property taxes

Wholesale Fee / Assignment Fee

This is your profit. It's the difference between the price you locked in with the seller and the price your buyer pays. If you put a property under contract for $120,000 and your buyer pays $135,000 for the contract, your assignment fee is $15,000.

Prospecting

Prospecting & Lead Generation

The lifeblood of your wholesaling business. Master these 25+ methods to find motivated sellers before anyone else does.

⭐
The Golden Rule of Prospecting
Never rely on just one lead source. The most successful wholesalers have 3–5 active prospecting channels running simultaneously. If one dries up, the others keep your pipeline full.

🚗 Driving for Dollars

Drive neighborhoods looking for distressed properties — overgrown lawns, boarded windows, roof damage, peeling paint, abandoned vehicles, mail piling up. Write down addresses or use an app like DealMachine to log them instantly.

Best for: Visual learners, people who want to know their market deeply. Cost: Gas + optional app subscription ($49–$99/mo). Tip: Pick a neighborhood and drive every street systematically. Set a daily goal of 10–20 new distressed addresses.

📞 Cold Calling

Pull lists of motivated seller leads from county records, PropStream, or BatchLeads, then call them directly. Cold calling has the highest conversion rate of any outreach method when done consistently. Expect to make 50–100 dials to have 5–10 real conversations.

Best for: People comfortable on the phone. Cost: Free to low cost. Tools: BatchDialer, CallTools, Mojo Dialer. Tip: Always call with a spirit of curiosity, not desperation. You're not begging — you're offering a solution.

📱 SMS / Text Blasting

Text messaging has a 98% open rate vs. ~20% for email. Send personalized texts to your lead lists asking if they've considered selling. Use SMS platforms like BatchLeads, Launch Control, or REIRail. Always comply with TCPA regulations — only text numbers where you have proper consent or a business reason.

🚫
TCPA Compliance Warning
The Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) regulates commercial text messaging. Violations can result in fines of $500–$1,500 per text. Use a compliant platform, honor opt-out requests immediately, and consult an attorney about your state's specific rules.

📬 Direct Mail

Send physical mailers (postcards, letters, yellow letters) to targeted lists. Direct mail is slower than digital methods but reaches sellers who may not respond to calls or texts. Best response rates come from consistent, repeated mailings — 3–7 touches over several months to the same list.

Best lists for direct mail: Absentee owners, tax delinquent, pre-foreclosure, probate, vacant properties. Cost: $0.40–$1.50 per piece including list, printing, and postage.

🚪 Door Knocking

Walk up to distressed properties and knock. This is highly effective because it shows initiative and creates a personal connection. Many sellers who ignore mail and don't answer calls will talk to you at the door. Always be respectful, professional, and ready to pivot if the person isn't the owner.

Best script opener: "Hi, I'm [Name] — I work with local investors who buy homes in this area. I noticed your home and wanted to see if you'd ever consider a cash offer?" See the full door-knocking script in Chapter 18.

📢 Bandit Signs

Small yard signs placed at intersections that say "We Buy Houses Cash — [Your Number]." Cheap and effective in many markets. Check local ordinances first — some cities prohibit them or restrict where they can be placed. Cost: $1–$3 per sign. Place at high-traffic intersections near distressed neighborhoods.

📘 Facebook (Free & Paid)

Free methods: Post in "For Sale by Owner" groups, "Buy/Sell/Trade" community groups, and local real estate investor groups. Message homeowners posting distressed properties. Offer to make a cash offer with a fast close.

Paid methods: Run Facebook ads targeting homeowners in distressed situations ("behind on mortgage," "need to sell fast," "facing foreclosure"). Budget $10–$30/day for lead generation ads with a simple landing page.

📸 Instagram

Post content that attracts motivated sellers: before-and-after transformations, "we buy houses" messaging, testimonials, and educational content. DM people posting about distressed properties or financial stress. Use location tags in your target market.

🎬 TikTok

Short-form video explaining wholesaling, showing distressed properties (with permission), and documenting deals. TikTok's algorithm favors organic reach — a video about "how to sell your house fast for cash" can reach thousands of local homeowners for free.

🎥 YouTube

Long-form video content about the home selling process, "sell your house fast" content, and market education. YouTube content compounds over time — videos published today can generate seller leads for years. Longer setup time but massive long-term ROI.

🏛️ County Records (Free Lead Source)

Your county assessor, treasurer, and recorder's office are goldmines of free data. Pull lists of:

  • Tax delinquent properties — owners who haven't paid property taxes (strong motivation to sell)
  • Code violation properties — cited for unsafe conditions or neglect
  • Probate filings — inherited properties that heirs may want to sell quickly
  • Notice of Default / Lis Pendens — early-stage foreclosure (highly motivated)
  • Absentee owner records — property tax bills mailed to a different address than the property

Skip Tracing

How to find contact information for property owners when their address is all you have.

What Is Skip Tracing?

Skip tracing is the process of locating a person's current contact information — phone numbers, email addresses, and mailing address — when you only have their name and/or property address. The term comes from the phrase "skipped town," referring to someone who has left without a forwarding address.

In wholesaling, you use skip tracing to find the phone numbers of property owners so you can call or text them about purchasing their property. For example, you might find 200 absentee owner addresses in county records, then skip trace all 200 to get their phone numbers for cold calling.

How Skip Tracing Works

  1. You collect a list of property addresses and owner names from county records or data platforms
  2. You upload this list to a skip tracing service
  3. The service cross-references its databases (public records, credit headers, utility records, social profiles) to find associated phone numbers and emails
  4. You receive an enhanced list with contact information
  5. You use that list to cold call, text, or email the owners
⚠️
Always Check the Do Not Call Registry
Before calling any skip-traced numbers, scrub them against the National Do Not Call (DNC) Registry. Calling DNC-registered numbers without prior consent can result in significant fines. Use a DNC scrubbing service or platforms like BatchLeads that do this automatically.

The 70% Rule

The most widely used formula in wholesaling and fix-and-flip investing:

The 70% Rule
MAO = (ARV × 70%) − Estimated Repairs
Your offer price to the seller should not exceed this number

The 30% buffer covers: your buyer's profit margin (typically 20%), closing costs, holding costs, and your assignment fee. By staying at or below MAO, you ensure there's enough margin in the deal for everyone to win.

Worked Example #1 — Light Rehab

🏠

Property: 3BR/1BA, 1,100 sq ft ranch home. Needs paint, flooring, fixtures, and landscaping.

Step 1 — Find ARV: Pull 3 comps of updated ranches within 0.5 miles. Comps sold at $165K, $172K, $168K. Average = $168,333. Use conservative ARV = $165,000

Step 2 — Estimate Repairs: Light cosmetic = ~$20/sq ft × 1,100 = $22,000

Step 3 — Calculate MAO: ($165,000 × 70%) − $22,000 = $115,500 − $22,000 = MAO: $93,500

Step 4 — Your offer to seller: Start at $85,000 (below MAO to leave room for negotiation). Final negotiated price: $90,000

Step 5 — Market to buyers at: $103,000–$108,000

Your assignment fee: $103,000 − $90,000 = $13,000 💰

Contracts

Getting Property Under Contract

Understanding the key components of a wholesale purchase agreement — without providing a contract.

🚫
Critical Reminder
This chapter explains what to look for in a purchase agreement — it does NOT provide a legal contract. Always use a state-specific purchase agreement reviewed by a licensed real estate attorney in your state. Never use generic contracts from the internet without attorney review.

What Is a Purchase Agreement?

A purchase agreement (also called a purchase and sale agreement or PSA) is a legally binding contract between a buyer and seller specifying the terms of a real estate purchase. When you get a property "under contract," this is the document you're signing. Your name goes in as the buyer — but you will ultimately assign your interest to a cash buyer before closing.

Key Components of a Wholesale Purchase Agreement

1. Assignment Clause

This is the most critical clause for wholesalers. It gives you the right to assign your interest in the contract to a third party (your cash buyer). Without this clause, you cannot legally wholesale the deal. Look for language like: "Buyer shall have the right to assign this contract to a third party without seller's consent."

2. Inspection Period / Due Diligence Period

A defined window of time (typically 7–21 days) during which you can inspect the property and back out for any reason without losing your earnest money. This protects you while you're finding a buyer and doing your due diligence. Negotiate the longest inspection period you can get — 14–21 days is ideal.

3. Earnest Money Deposit

A deposit provided to the seller (or held in escrow) showing good faith. For wholesale deals, keep this as low as possible while still making the deal credible. Common wholesale earnest money: $100–$1,000. Some deals require more — especially if the seller is sophisticated or working with an agent.

4. Purchase Price

The agreed-upon price you will pay for the property. This should be at or below your MAO, leaving room for your assignment fee. Remember: this is the price your buyer will ultimately pay to purchase the property (plus your assignment fee on top).

5. Closing Date

The date by which the transaction must close. In wholesale deals, you want enough time to find a buyer — typically 21–45 days from contract signing. Avoid closing dates shorter than 21 days unless your buyer is already lined up.

6. Contingencies

Conditions that must be met for the deal to close. Common contingencies in wholesale deals include the inspection/due diligence contingency (which gives you a way out) and sometimes a financing contingency for your buyer if they're using hard money.

This is where your fee gets locked in. Understand every step of the assignment process so nothing surprises you at the closing table.

What Is an Assignment?

When you assign a contract, you are transferring your rights and obligations under the purchase agreement to your buyer. You are not selling the property — you are selling your contractual interest in it. After the assignment, your buyer steps into your shoes and closes directly with the seller.

📄

1. Original Purchase Agreement — Between you (buyer) and the seller. Must include an assignment clause.

2. Assignment Agreement — Between you (assignor) and your cash buyer (assignee). Transfers your rights for the agreed assignment fee.

3. Proof of Funds / Buyer's Earnest Money — Your buyer typically provides this to the title company.

Double Closings

A powerful alternative to assignment when privacy or deal structure demands it.

What Is a Double Closing?

A double closing (also called a simultaneous closing or back-to-back closing) involves two separate transactions happening on the same day. First, you buy the property from the seller (Transaction A). Then, you immediately sell it to your cash buyer (Transaction B). Unlike an assignment, you actually take title to the property — for a matter of minutes or hours.

When to Use a Double Close Instead of Assignment

  • Your assignment fee is very large and you want to keep it private from the seller
  • The seller's contract doesn't allow assignment
  • The seller or their agent is uncomfortable with an assignment
  • Your buyer is using financing that requires them to purchase directly from the title holder (not from an assignee)
  • The deal involves a distressed seller who may back out if they see your profit margin

Transactional Funding

In a double close, you need to fund Transaction A (buying from seller) before Transaction B closes (selling to buyer). Since most wholesalers don't have the capital to buy the property outright — even briefly — this is where transactional funding comes in.

Transactional funders provide short-term loans (sometimes same-day) specifically for back-to-back closings. The loan is repaid the same day using the buyer's funds from Transaction B. Cost: typically 1–3% of the purchase price, or a flat fee of $500–$2,000.

Legal & Ethics

Do this right from day one. Legal and ethical wholesaling protects you, your sellers, and your reputation.

🚫
This Is Not Legal Advice
This section provides general educational information only. Laws vary dramatically by state. Always consult a licensed real estate attorney in your state before starting your wholesaling business.

Licensing Considerations

The #1 legal question in wholesaling: Do I need a real estate license?

The short answer is: it depends on your state. Traditionally, wholesalers have argued they don't need a license because they are selling their equitable interest in a contract — not the property itself. However, several states have moved to restrict or regulate wholesaling:

  • Illinois: Enacted laws in 2023 requiring disclosures and registration
  • Oklahoma: Has restrictions on marketing properties without a license
  • Wisconsin: Has its own specific regulations including Act 208 disclosures and licensee-as-principal disclosure requirements for licensed agents doing wholesale deals
  • Laws are evolving in many other states

Action item: Before you wholesale your first deal, consult a real estate attorney in your state who works with investors. This is non-negotiable.

Required Disclosures

Regardless of your state's licensing requirements, ethical wholesalers always disclose:

  • That you are buying the property with the intent to assign or resell
  • That you are a real estate licensee (if applicable) and have a financial interest in the transaction
  • That your offer is below market value and the seller has the right to seek other options
  • Any material facts about the property you are aware of that could affect the seller's decision

Marketing Honestly

  • Never claim to be a buyer if you intend to assign — be transparent about the process
  • Never misrepresent the property's value or the seller's options
  • Never use high-pressure tactics that exploit a seller's distress
  • Never advertise a property for sale publicly before you have it under contract
  • Be truthful about your experience, credentials, and company

Fair Housing Awareness

The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination in real estate transactions based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability. As a wholesaler:

  • Never target or avoid certain neighborhoods based on demographic composition
  • Apply the same marketing practices uniformly across all areas
  • Never make assumptions about a seller's motivation based on their race, ethnicity, or background
  • Be aware that certain practices — even if unintentional — can constitute fair housing violations

The Ethics of Wholesaling

Some people argue that wholesaling takes advantage of distressed sellers. Done dishonestly, it can. Done ethically, wholesaling provides genuine value — a fast, certain, hassle-free exit for people in difficult situations who would otherwise face foreclosure, costly repairs, or a drawn-out market listing.

The ethical wholesaler:

  • Presents offers honestly, explaining the trade-off between price and convenience
  • Gives sellers time to consider and encourages them to explore other options
  • Follows through on every commitment — never backs out without a contractual reason
  • Treats sellers with dignity and compassion regardless of their situation
  • Never ties up properties they have no intention of closing on

Analyzing & Offering

ARV (After Repair Value) is the estimated market value of a property after it has been fully renovated. Calculate it by finding 3–5 comparable sales (comps) of similar, updated homes within 0.5 miles that sold in the last 90 days. Average those sold prices, then adjust slightly based on differences in size, bedrooms, bathrooms, and condition. Be conservative — an overestimated ARV kills deals.

The 70% rule is the most widely used formula in fix-and-flip and wholesaling: MAO = (ARV × 70%) − Estimated Repairs. It means your maximum offer (including your assignment fee) should not exceed 70% of ARV after subtracting repairs. The 30% buffer covers your buyer's profit, closing costs, holding costs, and your fee.

Use cost-per-square-foot estimates: light cosmetic rehab ($15–$30/sqft), medium rehab with kitchen/bath ($30–$60/sqft), heavy gut rehab ($60–$100+/sqft). Learn to recognize what "light," "medium," and "heavy" rehab looks like by walking properties with investor buyers or contractors early in your wholesaling career. Get a local contractor to walk a few properties with you for calibration.

The 70% rule is a starting point, not a law. In hot markets with tight inventory, buyers may pay 75–80% of ARV. In slow markets or for major rehabs, they may only pay 60–65%. Know your buyers and what they require in your specific market. The best way to know: talk to your active cash buyers and ask them what formula they use.

Don't force a deal. If the seller won't accept a price that works for your buyer, you have three options: (1) walk away and follow up in 30 days, (2) negotiate on other terms like closing date or earnest money, or (3) explore creative structures like seller financing or lease options. A deal that doesn't work for your buyer is not a deal — it's a liability.

A good wholesale deal passes three tests: (1) your buyer can purchase at your price, renovate, sell at ARV, and still profit 15–20%, (2) your assignment fee is meaningful for your time and effort (typically $5K+), and (3) the title is clear and the timeline works. If all three pass, it's a deal worth pursuing.

Contracts & Closing

An assignment clause in your purchase agreement gives you the legal right to transfer (assign) your interest in the contract to a third party — your cash buyer. Without it, you cannot wholesale the deal. Look for language like: "Buyer reserves the right to assign this contract without seller approval." Have your attorney confirm it's present and enforceable in your state.

As a wholesaler, keep earnest money as low as possible while still being credible to the seller. $100–$1,000 is common for motivated sellers. Some deals require $2,000–$5,000 if the seller is sophisticated or working with a real estate agent. Negotiate hard on this — your earnest money is at risk if you can't find a buyer and need to back out.

You have a few options: (1) request a written extension from the seller (most motivated sellers will grant one if you communicate early), (2) use your inspection or due diligence contingency to exit the contract and recover your earnest money, or (3) if none of the above work, you may forfeit your earnest money. This is why having a solid buyers list before locking up deals is so important.

Use a double close when: your assignment fee is very large and you want to keep it private, the seller's contract prohibits assignment, or your buyer's financing requires them to purchase from the title holder directly. Double closings cost more (two sets of closing costs) and require transactional funding, but they solve specific structural problems that assignments cannot.

Transactional funding is a very short-term loan (often same-day) used specifically for back-to-back double closings. The lender funds your purchase (Transaction A) in the morning, and the loan is repaid from your buyer's funds when Transaction B closes the same day or shortly after. Typical cost: 1–3% of the loan amount or a flat fee.

Either party can propose the title company, but as a wholesaler you generally want to control this and use your investor-friendly title company. Write your preferred title company into your purchase agreement. This ensures the closing process is smooth and that the title company is familiar with assignment transactions.

Your assignment fee is disbursed by the title company at closing. The buyer brings the full funds (your seller's purchase price + your assignment fee). The title company pays the seller their contracted amount and issues you a check or wire for your assignment fee. Make sure this is clearly documented in both your purchase agreement and assignment agreement before closing.

Title insurance protects the buyer (and their lender) against losses from title defects — hidden liens, ownership disputes, recording errors, or fraud. As a wholesaler who never takes title, you don't typically need title insurance. Your cash buyer, however, should always get it. A good title company will automatically offer it as part of the closing.

Finding Buyers

Start with your county recorder's office — search for cash sales (transactions with no mortgage) in the last 12 months. Those buyers are your prospects. Then attend every local REIA meeting, join investor Facebook groups, and connect with hard money lenders. Post your deals consistently and ask every interested buyer for their contact info and buy box preferences.

A buy box is an investor's specific criteria for deals they want to purchase — property type, price range, geographic area, rehab level, and minimum profit margin. Knowing your buyers' buy boxes lets you instantly match deals to the right buyer and save everyone's time. Ask every buyer on your list to share their buy box when you first connect.

Proof of funds is documentation showing that a buyer has the financial ability to close the deal. For cash buyers, this is typically a recent bank or brokerage statement showing sufficient liquid funds. For hard money buyers, it's a pre-approval letter from their lender. Request it simply: "Before I send the deal package, can you email me proof of funds? Just a recent bank statement or lender letter works great."

Daisy-chaining is when another wholesaler pretends to be a buyer, takes your deal, and then tries to re-assign it to their own buyer — creating multiple assignments in a chain. This creates legal complexity, delays closings, and can collapse deals. Always ask: "Are you the end buyer or will you be assigning to someone else?" If they're also a wholesaler, work out a co-wholesale agreement upfront.

Both are valuable. Cash buyers are faster and more reliable — they can close in 7–14 days without any lender approval risk. Hard money buyers may close in 14–30 days and carry slightly more closing risk (if their lender doesn't approve the deal). For motivated sellers with tight timelines, prefer cash buyers. For standard deals with a 30-day window, hard money buyers are fine.

Business & Finance

Assignment fees are ordinary income, not capital gains (since you never held the property as an investment). They're taxed at your ordinary income rate. As a self-employed wholesaler, you'll also owe self-employment tax (~15.3%) on top of income tax. Set aside 25–35% of every fee for taxes and make quarterly estimated payments. Consult a CPA who works with real estate investors.

Your goal should be quality deals with healthy margins, not chasing volume at razor-thin fees. Shoot for a minimum $5,000 fee on most deals.

Many wholesalers have had sellers hang up on them multiple times before the same seller called them back months later ready to sell. Keep calling, keep logging, keep following up.

Don't apologize for your offer — explain it calmly and professionally. "I understand it's not the retail price you were hoping for, and I respect that. Wisconsin-Specific Questions

Wisconsin Act 208 established specific disclosure requirements for real estate licensees acting as buyers (principal capacity). If you are a licensed agent in Wisconsin and are buying or wholesaling properties, you have heightened disclosure obligations compared to unlicensed wholesalers. Consult a Wisconsin real estate attorney and review the WB-11 purchase contract with appropriate addenda before transacting. This guide is educational only — not legal advice.

Wisconsin's licensing laws for unlicensed wholesalers are evolving. Generally, selling your equitable interest in a contract (not the property itself) has been viewed as not requiring a license. However, publicly marketing a property for sale before closing could be considered brokerage activity requiring a license. Always consult a Wisconsin real estate attorney for the most current guidance on this topic.

Glossary

Every key term in real estate wholesaling — defined simply and clearly.

A
Absentee Owner
A property owner who does not live at the property they own. Their tax bill is mailed to a different address. Often a strong motivated seller prospect.
After Repair Value (ARV)
The estimated market value of a property after it has been fully renovated to current market standards. The most critical number in a wholesale deal analysis.
Assignment
The transfer of your rights and obligations under a purchase agreement to a third party (your buyer) in exchange for an assignment fee.
Assignment Agreement
The legal document formalizing the transfer of your contract rights to your cash buyer. Specifies the assignment fee and effective date.
Assignment Clause
Language in a purchase agreement that grants the buyer the right to assign the contract to another party. Critical for wholesale deals.
Assignment Fee
Your profit as a wholesaler. The difference between your locked-in seller price and what your buyer pays for the contract rights.
Assignee
The party receiving the contract rights in an assignment — your cash buyer.
Assignor
The party transferring contract rights in an assignment — you, the wholesaler.
B
Back-to-Back Closing
See Double Closing. Two transactions closing in sequence on the same day.
Bandit Signs
Small corrugated plastic yard signs placed at intersections advertising cash offers for homes. A low-cost, traditional wholesaling marketing tool.
Buyers List
A database of active cash investors who purchase wholesale deals. One of the most valuable assets a wholesaler can build.
Buy Box
An investor buyer's specific criteria for deals they will consider — property type, price range, location, condition, and minimum profit margin.
C
Cash Buyer
An investor who purchases real estate using cash (no mortgage), allowing for faster, more certain closings.
Chain of Title
The chronological record of all ownership transfers of a property. Clear chain of title is required for a clean closing.
Clear Title
A title to property free of liens, encumbrances, or ownership disputes. Required for a property to be sold.
Closing
The final step of a real estate transaction where ownership is transferred, all documents are signed, and funds are disbursed.
Closing Costs
Fees paid at closing by the buyer and/or seller. Includes title insurance, attorney fees, recording fees, transfer taxes, and prorations.
Cold Calling
Proactively calling potential motivated sellers without prior contact or relationship. One of the highest-converting prospecting methods in wholesaling.
Comps (Comparable Sales)
Recently sold properties similar to the subject property in location, size, style, and condition. Used to determine ARV.
Contingency
A condition in a purchase agreement that must be satisfied for the transaction to proceed. Common contingencies include inspection, financing, and title.
CRM (Customer Relationship Management)
Software used to track leads, follow-ups, and deal status. Essential for managing a high-volume wholesaling operation.
D
Daisy-Chaining
When another wholesaler poses as a buyer and re-assigns a deal to their own buyer. Can create legal complications and closing delays.
Deal Package
A summary document shared with buyers detailing a property's address, photos, ARV, estimated repairs, asking price, and profit potential.
Deed
A legal document that transfers ownership of real property from one party to another. Recorded with the county at closing.
Direct Mail
Sending physical marketing pieces (postcards, letters) to targeted lists of property owners. A classic and still-effective prospecting method.
Disposition
The process of selling or assigning a wholesale deal to a buyer. "Disposition" is the sell side of wholesaling.
Double Closing
A wholesaling structure involving two back-to-back closings — you buy from the seller, then immediately sell to your buyer. Keeps your assignment fee private.
Driving for Dollars
Physically driving neighborhoods to identify distressed properties as potential wholesale leads.
Due Diligence
The research and investigation a buyer performs before committing to purchase. Includes title search, property inspection, and market analysis.
Due Diligence Period
The contractually defined window during which a buyer can inspect the property and exit the contract without penalty. Often called the inspection period.
E
Earnest Money
A good-faith deposit paid by the buyer upon signing a purchase agreement. Shows the seller the buyer is serious. May be forfeited if the buyer backs out without cause.
Encumbrance
Any claim, lien, or liability attached to a property that may affect its value or transfer. Must be resolved before clear title can pass.
Equity
The difference between a property's market value and the total debt owed against it. A seller with significant equity has the most flexibility to accept a below-market cash offer.
Escrow
A neutral third-party arrangement where funds and documents are held until all conditions of a transaction are met.
Expired Listing
A property that was listed on the MLS but failed to sell before the listing agreement expired. Sellers of expired listings can be motivated — they've already tried and failed to sell through traditional channels.
F–G
Fair Housing Act
Federal law prohibiting discrimination in real estate transactions based on race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability.
Fix-and-Flip
An investment strategy where a property is purchased, renovated, and resold for a profit. The end buyer in most wholesale deals is a fix-and-flip investor.
FSBO (For Sale By Owner)
A property listed for sale directly by the owner without a real estate agent. Often a motivated seller prospect.
Geo Farming
A marketing strategy targeting a specific geographic area repeatedly with consistent outreach to build brand recognition and generate leads.
H–K
Hard Money Lender
A private lender providing short-term, asset-based loans to real estate investors. Loans based on property value rather than borrower creditworthiness.
Hard Money Loan
A short-term loan secured by real property, typically used by fix-and-flip investors. Higher interest rates but faster approval than conventional financing.
Inspection Period
See Due Diligence Period. A window of time for the buyer to inspect the property and exit without penalty.
KPI (Key Performance Indicator)
A measurable metric used to track business performance. In wholesaling: dials per day, conversations per day, offers per week, deals closed per month.
L–M
Lien
A legal claim against a property securing repayment of a debt. Must typically be paid off before or at closing. Types include mortgage liens, tax liens, mechanic's liens, and judgment liens.
Lis Pendens
A public notice that a lawsuit has been filed involving a property. Often signals an early-stage foreclosure proceeding — a prime motivated seller prospect.
MAO (Maximum Allowable Offer)
The highest price you can offer a seller while still leaving enough margin for your fee and your buyer's profit. Calculated as: (ARV × 70%) − Repairs.
Motivated Seller
A property owner who needs (not just wants) to sell quickly due to financial distress, life circumstance, or property condition. The core prospect in wholesaling.
MLS (Multiple Listing Service)
A database of properties listed for sale by real estate agents. Access typically requires a real estate license.
N–P
NOD (Notice of Default)
A formal notice filed by a lender indicating that a borrower has failed to make mortgage payments. The first formal step in foreclosure. Strong motivated seller indicator.
Probate
The legal process of settling a deceased person's estate. Inherited properties in probate are often sold quickly at a discount by heirs who don't want to manage them.
Proof of Funds (POF)
Documentation (bank statement, lender letter) verifying a buyer's ability to close a transaction. Always request before presenting a deal.
Purchase Agreement
A legally binding contract specifying the terms of a real estate purchase — price, closing date, contingencies, and earnest money. The core document in a wholesale deal.
R–S
REIA (Real Estate Investor Association)
Local organizations of real estate investors that meet regularly. Excellent for networking with buyers, sellers, and fellow wholesalers.
Rehab
Renovation work performed on a property to improve its condition and market value.
Skip Tracing
The process of finding contact information (phone, email) for property owners using their name and address. Used to identify ways to reach motivated sellers.
Subject Property
The specific property being analyzed or considered for a transaction.
T–Z
TCPA (Telephone Consumer Protection Act)
Federal law regulating commercial phone and text communications. Violations can result in significant fines. Always scrub lists against the Do Not Call registry.
Title
Legal ownership of a property. Clear title is required to sell a property.
Title Company
A company that handles the legal and administrative aspects of closing a real estate transaction — title search, escrow, document preparation, and fund disbursement.
Title Insurance
An insurance policy protecting a buyer (and lender) against losses arising from title defects — undisclosed liens, ownership disputes, or recording errors.
Title Search
A review of public records to verify a property's ownership history and identify any existing liens or encumbrances.
Transactional Funding
A very short-term loan used for double closings. The lender funds Transaction A; the loan is repaid from Transaction B proceeds the same day.
Wholesale Fee
See Assignment Fee. Your profit for finding and contracting a deal and connecting it with a buyer.
Wholesaling
A real estate investing strategy where a wholesaler finds a motivated seller, gets a property under contract, and then assigns that contract to a cash buyer for a fee — without ever purchasing or owning the property.
